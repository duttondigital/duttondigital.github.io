Twenty years ago, if you wanted to find a local business, you'd search the web. You'd find their website. You'd get their information directly from them.

Today, you scroll Instagram.

## Walled gardens everywhere

The open web — where anyone could publish anything and anyone could find it — is dying. In its place: platforms.

Facebook. Instagram. TikTok. Google Maps. Yelp. Each one a walled garden where businesses have to play by someone else's rules to be visible at all.

Want customers to find you? Better have an Instagram presence. Want to rank in local searches? Better keep your Google Business Profile pristine. Want younger customers? Better figure out TikTok.

Your own website? Increasingly irrelevant.

## The discovery problem

This shift fundamentally changes who controls the relationship between businesses and customers.

On the open web, you could optimise your site, build links, create content — and people would find you through search. You controlled your presence.

On platforms, you're subject to algorithms you don't understand and can't influence. One policy change, one algorithm update, and your visibility evaporates overnight.

Businesses that built their entire customer acquisition on Instagram have woken up to find their reach decimated because Meta decided to prioritise different content. They had no recourse. They didn't own the relationship.

## The homogenisation of everything

Platforms also flatten everything into the same format. Every business gets the same profile template. The same review system. The same limited ways to present themselves.

A quirky independent shop looks basically the same as a chain store. A family restaurant blends into the sea of food photos. Distinctiveness gets compressed into platform-approved boxes.

## What we lost

The open web was messy. Websites varied wildly in quality. Finding things took more effort. But you could build something unique. You could own your presence. You could create direct relationships with customers that no platform could take away.

You could also link freely, share openly, and build on what others created. The web was a network, not a series of isolated silos.

## Is there a way back?

Probably not to how things were. Platforms won because they solved real problems — discovery, trust, convenience. We can't pretend those problems don't exist.

But we can push back against complete dependence. Businesses can:

- **Maintain their own web presence** — Even if most traffic comes from platforms, having your own site means you always have somewhere to point people.
- **Own customer relationships** — Email lists, direct contact information. Things that don't disappear when a platform changes its rules.
- **Support open standards** — Structured data, RSS feeds, interoperable formats. The building blocks of a web that isn't controlled by a handful of companies.

None of this will bring back 2005. But it might prevent a future where a few platforms control all commercial visibility online.
