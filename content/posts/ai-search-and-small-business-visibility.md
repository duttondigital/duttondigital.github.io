"Find me a good electrician nearby."

Ten years ago, that meant typing into Google and browsing results. Five years ago, maybe asking Siri. Today, people are asking ChatGPT. And that changes everything about how businesses get discovered.

## The shift from links to answers

Traditional search gives you a list of options. You click through, evaluate, decide. The business with the best website, the best reviews, the best SEO — they had an advantage, but everyone at least had a chance to be seen.

AI search gives you an answer. One recommendation. Maybe a short list. The AI has already decided for you.

Where did that decision come from? How was your business evaluated? Why did the AI recommend your competitor instead of you?

Nobody knows. Including, often, the AI companies themselves.

## The black box problem

Search engines were never fully transparent, but there were rules you could learn. Keywords mattered. Links mattered. Page speed mattered. You could optimise.

AI recommendation systems are genuinely opaque. They're trained on vast datasets with biases nobody fully understands. They hallucinate — confidently stating things that aren't true. They can be influenced by how information appears in their training data, which might be years out of date.

Your business might be invisible to AI search because of factors completely outside your control. You might be recommended for the wrong reasons. You might be described inaccurately with no way to correct it.

## Who controls visibility now?

With traditional search, power was distributed (imperfectly) across many players. Your website mattered. Review platforms mattered. Local directories mattered. Google dominated, but wasn't the only path to discovery.

AI search concentrates power further. A handful of companies — OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, maybe a few others — will determine which businesses get recommended for which queries. Their training choices, their alignment decisions, their business partnerships will shape commercial visibility for millions of businesses.

That's an extraordinary amount of power with very little accountability.

## What can small businesses do?

Honestly? It's not clear yet. This is new territory.

Some things that probably help:

- **Structured data** — Machine-readable information about your business makes it easier for AI systems to understand and accurately represent you.
- **Accuracy everywhere** — Consistent, correct information across all platforms reduces the chance of AI confusion or hallucination about your business.
- **Direct relationships** — Customers who know you don't need AI to find you. Email lists, repeat business, word of mouth — these matter more than ever.
- **Distinctiveness** — Generic businesses blur together. Clear identity makes you memorable to humans and potentially more distinguishable to AI.

But fundamentally, small businesses are about to become dependent on AI systems they don't understand and can't influence. That's concerning.

## The bigger picture

This isn't just a business problem. It's a power problem.

When a few AI systems mediate how people discover everything — businesses, information, services, products — those systems become critical infrastructure. Who builds them, who controls them, whose interests they serve: these become questions with enormous societal stakes.

We're sleepwalking into this. Most people don't realise how much AI already shapes what they see and find. By the time the implications are clear, the systems will be entrenched.

Worth thinking about.
