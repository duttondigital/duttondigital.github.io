Every company builds their own website. Every company implements their own version of accessibility. And every user is forced to adapt to whatever each company decided was "good enough."

This is broken.

## The current model

Right now, accessibility is something each business handles individually. A restaurant builds a website. A shop builds a website. A ticket company builds a website. Each one makes their own choices about fonts, colours, contrast, navigation, screen reader support — the list goes on.

Some do it well. Most don't. And even the ones that try often fall short because implementing proper accessibility is genuinely difficult. It requires expertise, testing, and ongoing maintenance that most businesses simply can't afford.

The result? A web that's inconsistent, frustrating, and frequently unusable for people who need accessibility features most.

## It's not just accessibility

The same problem applies to tracking, advertising, and user experience in general. When you visit a website, you're operating entirely within parameters set by that company. You accept their cookies or you don't use the site. You see their ads. You navigate their way.

Users have almost no control over their own experience.

## A better approach

What if we decoupled the data from the presentation?

Instead of every business building their own website with their own accessibility implementation, imagine a world where businesses publish structured data — their opening hours, their menu, their events, their products — and users choose how to view it.

Your device. Your preferences. Your accessibility settings. Applied universally, to every business, every time.

A visually impaired user wouldn't need to hope that each individual restaurant implemented proper screen reader support. Their preferred reader would just work, because it's reading standardised data, not navigating a bespoke website.

Someone who needs high contrast wouldn't be stuck with whatever colour scheme a designer picked. They'd see everything in their chosen format.

## The real benefit

This isn't just about making things easier for users with disabilities — though that alone would be reason enough. It's about raising the floor.

When accessibility is implemented once, at the platform level, it can be done properly. It can be tested thoroughly. It can be maintained consistently. The standard becomes genuinely high, not a patchwork of half-measures.

And businesses benefit too. They don't need to become accessibility experts. They just need to publish accurate data.
